    Wisdom of the crowds: Peer review edition

    My acquaintance Walter Hickey, from FiveThirtyEight, made an interesting comment that I wanted to address in over 140 characters.

    @becky_koenig @kadesoto also, readers, commenters and other publications are almost certainly more grilling than a peer review.

    — Walter Hickey (@WaltHickey) April 9, 2014

    It's an interesting idea to think about. At face value the idea makes sense -- a million people out there, any number of which have the time, energy, and expertise to evaluate your writing critically. But I'm not sure the evidence is out there suggesting that people actually do that. When I read critiques of news articles, they seem partisan or opinion based -- "I think this article is wrong because I disagree with you." Peer review, at its best, does more than this. Readers and commenters will be quick to pick up on and take down obvious flaws and logical fallacies. But can you count on them to investigate statistical analyses, interpretations of scientific theory? Do your readers know how to bootstrap, score psychological measures, or do a shift of benchwork?

    A few of them do, but it's irresponsible to assume that those people are out there and are willing and motivated to clean up any mistakes you made. Scientific peer review, despite its faults, ensures that at least some wise brains have the professional responsibility to evaluate your arguments. Ideally, it matches up subject matter experts -- people who can bootstrap and evaluate theory, to continue the example -- to papers that require it.

    Data journalists would be wise to seek out impartial reviewers instead of assuming that readers, commenters, and other publications will do the job of ensuring accuracy for them.

    This interesting issue raises a larger point that I have been concerned about for a few weeks now.     Notes on Migo, Mayes, and Montaldi (2012)

    Getting around to reading this neat review of the remember/know procedure written by Migo, Mayes, and Montaldi (2012) (). It covers some interesting issues in the use of remember/know judgments as well as some best practices. Here are some assorted notes:

    • p. 1437: A messy figure good for showing the complicated confidence/RKG/memory interrelationships

    • p. 1439: Distinctions between remember/know and source memory tasks

    • p. 1441: The beginning of a helpful section relating remember/know to confidence

    • p. 1442: Citation to Smith, Wixted, and Squire (2011), which I should add to the reading list

    • p. 1443: Discussion of how "false recollection" can be due to "true," yet mis-connected, recollections

    The authors also outline some valuable guidelines for working with RKG judgments in the laboratory:

    • Include the exact instructions in the manuscript

    • Describe any instruction checks used during the experiment

    • Describe any instruction checks used after the experiment

    • Declare any rules for elimination or replacing of subjects

    • Include "the measurement assumption used to calculate familiarity performance" (p. 1446)

    Most of these are self-evident, but it's good to see them in print. Later on (p. 1446) there's a discussion of how the Rajaram (1993) instructions suggest that know responses can only be made when a subject is certain an item was presented. This isn't true, and Montaldi et al. (2006) discusses this further.

    • p. 1448: An interesting suggestion that subjects who have remember rates over 50% should be excluded

    • p. 1449: A recommendation against using guess responses -- oops!

    • p. 1449: Some criticisms of Ingram et al. (2012), which my work is partially inspired by

    Bottom line, a valuable article that'll be added to the project folder.

    Examining item characteristics

    So, in my dissertation work, subjects study some items and take a test on studied and unstudied items. When subjects indicate an item was "old," or previously studied, they judge whether they remember, know, or guess that the item is old. Remember responses are provided when subjects can mentally recollect the moment of the item's occurrence in the list. Know responses are made when subjects think they saw the item on the list but can't recollect presentation details. Last, subjects are told to make a guess response when they're just guessing.

    It also happens that the items are categorized words that vary in terms of response frequency, which is a sort of typicality. Common category members (like eagle from the birds category) have a low response frequency (e.g., 1 or 2), whereas less common category members have a high response frequency.

    We wondered if subjects might be much more likely to respond remember to a high response frequency item than a low response frequency item, and likewise for know and guess. To investigate I took the average response frequency for each subjects' remember, know, and guess responses. I divided these into correct responses (i.e., hits) and incorrect responses (i.e., false alarms), and here's what we get:

    Not much of a difference. I haven't run any significance tests on these data but there's nothing shocking -- in other words, subjects seem equally likely to respond remember, whether correctly or incorrectly, to items of all output dominance values.

    Exploring issues of empty cells

    In our confidence-accuracy relationship research, one analysis we're interested in is called the between-events confidence-accuracy correlation. This between-events analysis describes the extent to which events or items (e.g., words) that are responded to with greater confidence are also more likely to be responded to correctly. My dissertation research aims to obtain between-events correlations (as well as correlations of other types) for items responded to with remember, know, or guess after an "old" recognition decision (read up on the remember/know procedure for some background here).

    To get this particular confidence-accuracy correlation, we take each item and get an average confidence and average accuracy for each, then plot the trendline.

    The problem, though, is that there are some items which only receive a recognition decision of "old" a small number of times in the study. The objective in this analysis was to exclude those items for which only a few "old" responses were given and see if the resulting confidence-accuracy correlations differ dramatically.

    To do this, I filtered out items for which there were fewer than 2, 6, or 11 observations and recomputed the confidence-accuracy correlations.

    Here are the results:

    The good news is that there are no big changes. Most items get at least 10 remember responses and most also get 5 know responses. Most items also get 1 guess response. Regardless of the number of items which are taken out, too, the confidence-accuracy relationships are remaining steady.

You can take a look at the code -- mostly spliced together through different tutorials -- on my Github. It takes a reading of the light in the apartment every 15 seconds and uploads it to Xively, which handles the visualization. Here's what it looks like over the last three days:

    We see a neat, regular pattern. I'm not exactly sure what the general patterns indicate -- this is all hypothesis at this point -- but we see a peak in the morning when I get ready for work or the day with the lights on and maximum brightness. In fact, I think you can even tell the unit's in a room with eastward facing windows -- a big blast of sun as it rises, followed by a general decrease in light over the day.
