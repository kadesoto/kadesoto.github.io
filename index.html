    Examining item characteristics

    So, in my dissertation work, subjects study some items and take a test on studied and unstudied items. When subjects indicate an item was "old," or previously studied, they judge whether they remember, know, or guess that the item is old. Remember responses are provided when subjects can mentally recollect the moment of the item's occurrence in the list. Know responses are made when subjects think they saw the item on the list but can't recollect presentation details. Last, subjects are told to make a guess response when they're just guessing.

    It also happens that the items are categorized words that vary in terms of response frequency, which is a sort of typicality. Common category members (like eagle from the birds category) have a low response frequency (e.g., 1 or 2), whereas less common category members have a high response frequency.

    We wondered if subjects might be much more likely to respond remember to a high response frequency item than a low response frequency item, and likewise for know and guess. To investigate I took the average response frequency for each subjects' remember, know, and guess responses. I divided these into correct responses (i.e., hits) and incorrect responses (i.e., false alarms), and here's what we get:

    Not much of a difference. I haven't run any significance tests on these data but there's nothing shocking -- in other words, subjects seem equally likely to respond remember, whether correctly or incorrectly, to items of all output dominance values.

    Do you agree with my interpretation?

    Exploring issues of empty cells

    In our confidence-accuracy relationship research, one analysis we're interested in is called the between-events confidence-accuracy correlation. This between-events analysis describes the extent to which events or items (e.g., words) that are responded to with greater confidence are also more likely to be responded to correctly. My dissertation research aims to obtain between-events correlations (as well as correlations of other types) for items responded to with remember, know, or guess after an "old" recognition decision (read up on the remember/know procedure for some background here).

    To get this particular confidence-accuracy correlation, we take each item and get an average confidence and average accuracy for each, then plot the trendline.

    The problem, though, is that there are some items which only receive a recognition decision of "old" a small number of times in the study. The objective in this analysis was to exclude those items for which only a few "old" responses were given and see if the resulting confidence-accuracy correlations differ dramatically.

    To do this, I filtered out items for which there were fewer than 2, 6, or 11 observations and recomputed the confidence-accuracy correlations.

    Here are the results:

    The good news is that there are no big changes. Most items get at least 10 remember responses and most also get 5 know responses. Most items also get 1 guess response. Regardless of the number of items which are taken out, too, the confidence-accuracy relationships are remaining steady.

    Another little side project: I rigged together an Arduino, an Ethernet Shield, and a photometer in the apartment. You can take a look at the code -- mostly spliced together through different tutorials -- on my Github. It takes a reading of the light in the apartment every 15 seconds and uploads it to Xively, which handles the visualization. Here's what it looks like over the last three days:

    We see a neat, regular pattern. I'm not exactly sure what the general patterns indicate -- this is all hypothesis at this point -- but we see a peak in the morning when I get ready for work or the day with the lights on and maximum brightness. In fact, I think you can even tell the unit's in a room with eastward facing windows -- a big blast of sun as it rises, followed by a general decrease in light over the day. I think some of the noise is due to different lights that are being turned on and off in the room, but also cloudiness. The third day worth of data is much smoother. I wonder if this means the day was sunnier (i.e., less cloudy) and I messed around less with the lights.

    Anything else I ought to test? Eventually, I should move the unit into my office, which has windows that face to the south. I bet you'd find the light pattern to be much more normally distributed.

    Experience sampling data

    A break from your regularly scheduled program -- here's a new entry in Personal Data Friday:

    Over the last month I've been using Reporter, an experience sampling iPhone app designed by personal analytics czar Nicholas Felton that probes me at random times during my waking hours to determine what I'm up to and who I'm with. Experience sampling is a technique that's been used for a long time in the autobiographical memory literature (e.g., Csikszentmihalyi & Larson, 1987 ()), so I was excited to find an opportunity to apply the technique to my own life. The idea is that over time, the data recorded at random intervals during the day begins to approximate a clear snapshot of one's entire life. Of course, with a limited handful of data points, this method of estimation may not be particularly accurate.

    Another neat finding

    In a meeting with Roddy yesterday he suggested I conduct a conceptual replication of an Ingram, Mickes, and Wixted (2012) () study in which the authors showed that know judgments made with high confidence are, on average, more likely to be correct than remember judgments made with lower confidence. This is an interesting finding because it means that remember judgments aren't functionally equivalent to memories held with high confidence. This is stated well in the title of this paper: "Recollection (i.e., as indexed by remember judgments) can be weak and familiarity (as indexed by knows) can be strong."

    So I divided up all "old" recognition decisions by whether they were remembered, known, or guessed and looked at their average accuracy across confidence bins of 0-24, 25-49, 50-74, and 75-100. And here's what we get:

    Sure enough, my findings support the discoveries of Ingram and colleagues. Know judgments made with high (i.e., 76-100) confidence are more likely to be correct than remember judgments made with lower (i.e., 26-50) confidence.

I finished the work this morning and now know the within-item correlations for remember, know, and guess responses.     Review of the Jawbone UP24, about four months in

    My buddy (and favorite Chemicals Professional) Bennett reports he's interested in a fitness tracker. I've been using my Jawbone UP 24 for about four months now, so it's a good time to write about it.

    First, some background: I got burned by being an early adopter in this area about a year and a half ago. I went through six different copies of the Jawbone UP, v. 1, which was truly a horrible device. Once the pain of that experience faded, I tried some other trackers -- the Larklife (which I think has been discontinued) and the Fitbit Flex (which I wrote about here). But I finally got an UP24 and have been very happy with it since. Most tech writers seem to favor the Fitbit Force (assuming it isn't giving you horrible skin rashes), but I think the UP24 is an under appreciated piece of tech. Here's why.

    Aesthetics and comfort. Let's get this out of the way first: I think the UP24 is one of the best-looking fitness trackers out there. The orange ("persimmon") is what I have, and the black is also a solid choice. If you go with the standard UP (to save $50), there are other nice colors available. These things look more like bracelets or fashion accessories than connected devices. They're also comfortable, too -- this is something you're buying to wear, literally, 24 hours a day (hence the name) -- so you can't understate the importance of this issue. The only times the UP24 gets uncomfortable or weird is when I'm pulling my arm out from underneath a pillow in the middle of the night. It's super easy to put on, take off, and charge. (Bennett is a swimmer, though, and the device is not waterproof. Rather, it's described as "showerproof.")

    Fitness tracking. Want to record a workout with your UP24? Press the band a few times and it goes into stopwatch mode. When you're done, press it again to turn it off. If your workout was a run, you're basically done. If you did something else that the UP24 can't sense (e.g., bike riding, cross training), you go into the app and tell it what you did. Distance, time, calories burned, and so on are all baked in, and you can edit the intensity of your workout to fine-tune what's reported. I mostly use my UP24 to measure running workouts, so the device especially excels. The pedometer is generally accurate at measuring distance assuming I'm running at my usual slow pace. If I try to speed up or slow down even further, the estimates can get off (but never by more than half a mile or so). The biggest downside of the UP24 is that there's no screen on it, though, so you have no idea how far you've run while you're running. This means that before a workout, I'll have to map out my route and try and recall it during the run to make sure I do the right distance.

    Sleep tracking. One of the main reasons I got the UP24 was to track my sleep. This hasn't been as useful as I thought it would be (although you can view my own public sleep data by clicking here -- although it seems to have broken, and I don't know why). Jawbone released a new UP Coffee app in the interests of helping users discover correlations between their daily activity and sleep habits, but knowing what we know about statistics, this sort of stuff requires a whole lot of data to be collected before "insights" become accurate.

    Interconnectivity. Honestly, this is what makes the Jawbone UP24 the winner for me. Jawbone has spent more effort than any other fitness tracker has to ensure interconnectivity and interoperability between different "internet of things" devices. Here's what I mean. When I wake up, the living room lights turn on. When I hop on my scale, the data is sent directly to the UP app. Everything is simultaneously copied to my Google Drive, too. I could set up even more triggers, thanks to IFTTT (no other tracker supports this, to my knowledge), to text me when I hit a step limit, send an e-mail or a tweet if I've had a lazy day, or write a blog post if I report that I'm sad. I find this reassuring because it means that when it becomes time to try a new fitness tracker, I can take my data with me (perhaps with a bit of wrangling). Considering that personal fitness tracking has the potential to be a lifelong hobby, an eye toward archival and longevity is huge.

    App. I like the Jawbone app a lot, too. I'm not going to get into it here, though. One warning: These guys seem to build for the iPhone first.

    The bottom line: The Jawbone UP24 is a comfortable, fashion conscious wristband that provides a (relatively) inexpensive way to start collecting personal data. I'm happy with mine and love taking it on runs, and am hugely grateful that the device connects to so many other devices and services that I love. When something comes out with more and more accurate sensors and a screen (like some of Samsung's new offerings, perhaps), it might be time to upgrade. But for now, I don't foresee taking this thing off for the rest of the year at least.
