Another neat finding

In a meeting with Roddy yesterday he suggested I conduct a conceptual replication of an Ingram, Mickes, and Wixted (2012) () study in which the authors showed that know judgments made with high confidence are, on average, more likely to be correct than remember judgments made with lower confidence. This is an interesting finding because it means that remember judgments aren't functionally equivalent to memories held with high confidence. This is stated well in the title of this paper: "Recollection (i.e., as indexed by remember judgments) can be weak and familiarity (as indexed by knows) can be strong."

So I divided up all "old" recognition decisions by whether they were remembered, known, or guessed and looked at their average accuracy across confidence bins of 0-24, 25-49, 50-74, and 75-100. And here's what we get:

Sure enough, my findings support the discoveries of Ingram and colleagues. Know judgments made with high (i.e., 76-100) confidence are more likely to be correct than remember judgments made with lower (i.e., 26-50) confidence.

Redoing a figure

Every once in a while we researchers have the pleasure of recreating a historic or classic figure for reuse in a presentation or paper. It's a zenlike experience to engage in this process of re-transcription. Today's mission was to redo Figure 1 from a 1962 paper by Bennet Murdock () on the serial position curve in memory.

Here's the original figure...

... and here's my redo.

Although the new figure is much more flexible (can be edited, etc.) and vector rather than raster (can be resized, reshaped, etc., without loss of quality), there's a real elegance to the original figure. In particular, I bumped into issues recreating the "shiny" bubbles of the 20-1 condition in the original, so I had to settle for a less interesting (but perhaps more readable) intermediate gray shade. Additionally, it's not so easy to create those "legend arrows" from the original, so I stuck with a more traditional legend.

What did the original figure creator get right? What did he or she get wrong? I was about to assume the figure was Murdock's work, but it occurred to me that the journal typesetter or editor may have created the figure. I think I have seen similar figures in papers by other authors, but am not certain.

In order to indicate this, I enter a little tag I invented myself -- [ paywall ] -- and Fargo renders a dollar sign icon. Later today I'll link the icon to some information about paywalls.
