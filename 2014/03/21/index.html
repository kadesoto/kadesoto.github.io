Experience sampling data

A break from your regularly scheduled program -- here's a new entry in Personal Data Friday:

Over the last month I've been using Reporter, an experience sampling iPhone app designed by personal analytics czar Nicholas Felton that probes me at random times during my waking hours to determine what I'm up to and who I'm with. Experience sampling is a technique that's been used for a long time in the autobiographical memory literature (e.g., Csikszentmihalyi & Larson, 1987 ()), so I was excited to find an opportunity to apply the technique to my own life. The idea is that over time, the data recorded at random intervals during the day begins to approximate a clear snapshot of one's entire life. Of course, with a limited handful of data points, this method of estimation may not be particularly accurate.

So, let's take a look at a random sample of daily activities:

You can see I've spent a lot of time working on the computer, but a similar amount of time socializing (hanging out with friends, being with people, etc.). A disappointing amount of my time is spent waiting.

To answer the question "Where are you?" I most often report:

Basically at home or at work. Home beats out the Psychology Building by a fair margin here because I have been working from home a bit more than usual lately. You can see that I haven't spent a significant amount of time anywhere else -- everything else is basically individual observations.

And last but not least, who I'm with:

Lots of alone time, lots of significant other time, then it's down to buddies. Becky and I spend a lot of time together, clearly.

Unfortunately, I don't think I can keep this project up much longer -- it's a little too disruptive to do and I don't like the switch you have to flip to go to bed at the end of the day (I keep forgetting to toggle it, so get polled during sleeping hours by accident). It was a neat project while it lasted, though!
