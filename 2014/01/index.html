Prepared some notes for tomorrow's meeting

I just finished something relatively uninteresting: Repackaging many of the figures I've shared over the last month so Roddy and I can talk about them at our meeting. Doing this reminded me of a thought I've had several times before: It is a shame that researchers must use two-dimensional mediums like tables and figures to represent a set of data. Thinking about data would be much easier if you could control analyses via natural language processing. For instance, you say to your Excel file, "Show me the false alarm rate for the word 'cardinal' in Experiment 1" and the number pops up -- boom, 0.19. Instead what you have to when discussing data is think of every possible way folks will want to consider the dataset and construct a table or figure for each way. That's not very convenient, especially when 90% of them will eventually go unused!

(Something like Wolfram Alpha for personal use or personal datasets would be close to what I'm thinking about.)

Oh well, give it five or 10 years and maybe we'll be there.

Some initial thoughts about Google Glass

I've had a tangerine-colored pair of Google Glass for five days now and thought I would share some initial impressions.

Downside: First, although I'm platform agnostic (psychologically speaking), I am set up in "Apple Land," not "Google World." It is a big change of pace to think of Google Contacts, Hangouts, and Google+ instead of iMessages and iChat. As a result a lot of functionality is unclear to me. If I take a picture and share it with a friend, I have no idea how they're receiving it -- via SMS? through a Hangout? Is it sending an e-mail? It's very strange. I'll text a friend later and ask, "Did you get that picture I sent you?" and most of the times they didn't. Who knows where it went.

Upside: Having something on your face that can take pictures is really cool. Being able to quickly take photos with a wink, a button press, or a head spasm-plus-voice command is convenient. It's nice to get a natural framing too, so I can show to others: "This is how something looks when I look at it."

Here's an example -- a photo I took of the Cathedral Basilica of St. Louis while walking home from the bus stop yesterday. I was bundled up with hat and gloves and didn't need to undo any of that to take a quick picture.

Downside: Battery life. I don't understand it and it makes me nervous. It was really awful the first few days but seems to have mellowed out. Maybe it's finished installing updates, Glassware, etc. and is happy just to coexist with me?

Upside: Having a Bluetooth headset. This is basic, but I've never made a phone call wirelessly before. Being able to dial someone in my pocket and speak to them with Google Glass is nice. Call quality (and reception) has varied, though. The same walk I photographed the Basilica I tried to make a call that dropped several times in a row. I don't know much about Bluetooth but it seems like a relatively fragile medium.

Mixed bag: The looks. Privately, I enjoy my bright orange Google Glass and they remind me of the future. When I go out into the wild world, though, I have no idea what everyone's thinking. (Well, I know what they're thinking!) A photo of the author as a Glasshole:

Downside: As an iPhone user I feel the functionality is quite limited. It costs an arm and a leg to tether (and it's not something I can even do with my current mobile data plan -- AT&T with unlimited data). Which means the thing sits inert on my face for most of the time, eventually bugging my ears.

Upside: There is something strangely natural and reassuring about a face-mounted display. This sounds ridiculous, but even after a few days it feels natural to have them on -- almost comfortable. It's a form factor that seems goofy to an outsider but maybe isn't so strange after all. I guess as someone who has worn glasses since third grade, it's not that out there.

Downside: Terrified about what would happen if I brought them into the bathroom by accident.

More dissertation analyses

Continuing from where I left off yesterday.

I looked at the correlation between false alarm rate and response frequency, like we did in the Psychological Science paper. The figure looks exactly like I'd expect it to (if anything, the correlation is a little weak).

I also tried to look at false remember, know, and guess responses as a function of response frequency and it was pretty messy. I think I need more power in order to look at those functions.

More concerning, though, are the between-event confidence-accuracy correlations from yesterday. Remember that there were lots of values at the ceiling and the floor (refer to the figure from yesterday if you like). Because I've included unrelated lures in that analysis -- which were never studied by anyone -- I have data points in there that would never ever come off the floor. Specifically, any R, K, or G response to an unrelated lure would be located at the point (confidence, 0). So the solution is to recompute the confidence-accuracy relation for these items omitting unrelated lures. And when I do that, the results aren't quite as pretty as they were yesterday -- dang.

Without doing any stats, the correlation for remembered items (r = .24) appears similar to the correlation for known items (r = .25). Both, though, are better than the correlation for items that are guessed (r = .03).

As you can see in this figure, making this change pulls a lot of points off the floor, especially for remember responses. This hasn't happened yet for K and G -- I am reasonably certain this is a power thing. (Reasonably.)

Dissertation (Project 15) analyses

Working on analyses for Experiment 2 of my dissertation. I have 12 subjects' worth of pilot data that I'm investigating today to ensure there's nothing funky going on with my data collection, task, and so forth. So what I'm looking at is very basic stuff -- i.e., mean-level -- just to make sure nothing is going astray.

Here are two tables that indicate the frequency with which subjects say "old" to the three different types of items in the study and the confidence with which they offer those "old" responses. That's on the left. On the right, I show the likelihood with which people say "remember," "know," and "guess" to the different item types.

Looking at these tables alerts me that I could make yet a third one showing confidence for "remember," "know," and "guess." I'll do that in just a minute, although it's likely that they'll show anything unsurprising.

Here's that table. A possible three-way interaction?

Next I have scatterplots depicting the between-events (top) and between-subjects (bottom) confidence-accuracy correlations. First we see a lot of ceiling and floor data for the between-events plots. This is expected because when we collapse across items, there really aren't a lot of subjects to drive variability. This would look a lot better with five or 10 times as many subjects.

(Note: I noticed a small issue with the between-subjects figures that I've corrected in a later version -- does not change the conclusions, though.)

The bottom looks great, though. Subject-level confidence-accuracy plots are low on points but seem to have plenty of variability, which means we can trust the resulting Pearson correlations.

And what are those Pearson correlations, you might ask? Exactly what I was hoping. The confidence-accuracy relation for remembered items (r = .54) is higher than the relation for known items (r = .28) is higher than the relation for guessed items (r = .07) from a between-events perspective.

The same relation holds between subjects: The correlation for remembered items (r = .91) > the correlation for known items (r = .65) > the correlation for guessed items (r = .56).

So far, it's a really nice dataset and will look better with the full contingent of subjects.

This all lives in "1-27-14 Data.xlsx."

01/27/14; 12:52:17 PM
Met with Jason

I met with Jason today to get a sanity check on some of the dissertation (Project 15) work I've been up to lately. He suggests everything's looking good so far. It's important to visualize my data first through use of scatterplots, etc. Then, I should perhaps separate analyses by remember/know/guess and conduct "simple" logistic regression on the confidence-accuracy relationships. Then you can compare beta weights via t-tests.

You can do this for each subject and average the betas, or you can just get one big equation. Given the data it's probably the case you'd need to average.

If not, we get into the land of multilevel modeling or linear mixed modeling. These methods are able to account for differences among subjects but deal with issues of missing data.

Fitting power functions

I've spent most of my time and energy today trying to fit forgetting functions to one of my datasets. Research (e.g., Wixted & Ebbesen, 1991) suggests that the power function (i.e., y = at^-b) best describes forgetting from memory. I am not experienced in fitting these functions, though, so I had Excel draw power function trendlines through my data and just copied the resulting numbers. I am not super confident that I'm getting the right ones, though, so I really need to take things to a statistical package. Problem is, I don't know which one is right for the job.

Plus, Adam brought up a good point when we spoke today: The data that I am trying to fit might involve a special case in which the power function (or really any function that drops off steeply after t = 0) might not best describe the underlying psychological mechanism I am exploring.

It's really neat taking a look at forgetting for different items, though. Here's a vague, unlabeled figure so you can get a sense of what I'm talking about! I hope to be able to discuss some of these details with you soon.

Time for me to take a break and focus on some other work, I think.
