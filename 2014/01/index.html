Wednesday, January 29, 2014
Prepared some notes for tomorrow's meeting#

I just finished something relatively uninteresting: Repackaging many of the figures I've shared over the last month so Roddy and I can talk about them at our meeting. Doing this reminded me of a thought I've had several times before: It is a shame that researchers must use two-dimensional mediums like tables and figures to represent a set of data. Thinking about data would be much easier if you could control analyses via natural language processing. For instance, you say to your Excel file, "Show me the false alarm rate for the word 'cardinal' in Experiment 1" and the number pops up -- boom, 0.19. Instead what you have to when discussing data is think of every possible way folks will want to consider the dataset and construct a table or figure for each way. That's not very convenient, especially when 90% of them will eventually go unused!#

(Something like Wolfram Alpha for personal use or personal datasets would be close to what I'm thinking about.)#

Oh well, give it five or 10 years and maybe we'll be there.#

Some initial thoughts about Google Glass#

I've had a tangerine-colored pair of Google Glass for five days now and thought I would share some initial impressions.#

Downside: First, although I'm platform agnostic (psychologically speaking), I am set up in "Apple Land," not "Google World." It is a big change of pace to think of Google Contacts, Hangouts, and Google+ instead of iMessages and iChat. As a result a lot of functionality is unclear to me. If I take a picture and share it with a friend, I have no idea how they're receiving it -- via SMS? through a Hangout? Is it sending an e-mail? It's very strange. I'll text a friend later and ask, "Did you get that picture I sent you?" and most of the times they didn't. Who knows where it went. #

Upside: Having something on your face that can take pictures is really cool. Being able to quickly take photos with a wink, a button press, or a head spasm-plus-voice command is convenient. It's nice to get a natural framing too, so I can show to others: "This is how something looks when I look at it." #

Here's an example -- a photo I took of the Cathedral Basilica of St. Louis while walking home from the bus stop yesterday. I was bundled up with hat and gloves and didn't need to undo any of that to take a quick picture.#

#

Downside: Battery life. I don't understand it and it makes me nervous. It was really awful the first few days but seems to have mellowed out. Maybe it's finished installing updates, Glassware, etc. and is happy just to coexist with me?#

Upside: Having a Bluetooth headset. This is basic, but I've never made a phone call wirelessly before. Being able to dial someone in my pocket and speak to them with Google Glass is nice. Call quality (and reception) has varied, though. The same walk I photographed the Basilica I tried to make a call that dropped several times in a row. I don't know much about Bluetooth but it seems like a relatively fragile medium.#

Mixed bag: The looks. Privately, I enjoy my bright orange Google Glass and they remind me of the future. When I go out into the wild world, though, I have no idea what everyone's thinking. (Well, I know what they're thinking!) A photo of the author as a Glasshole:#

Upside: There is something strangely natural and reassuring about a face-mounted display. This sounds ridiculous, but even after a few days it feels natural to have them on -- almost comfortable. Wednesday, January 15, 2014
Continuing to puzzle over R/K/G#

So I've gone back to some of the original remember/know papers and have plotted the data in a way that's a bit more traditional. Here's what they look like.#

#

I've been worrying about whether performance under 50% indicates performance worse than chance, but I think I've been going about it wrong. What I need to do, basically, is subtract, say, remember hits from remember false alarms to get discrimination.#

But right away you see the problem -- the base rates are uneven; there are 100 targets, 100 related lures, and 100 unrelated lures. So which lure false alarm rate do you subtract from the hit rate? You could subtract one or the other, or you could also average both and subtract that from the hit rate.#

And if you do that, remember d' = 1.43, know d' = 0.32, and guess d' = -0.11. The negative d' for remembering is not altogether concerning. Gardiner, Ramponi, and Richardson-Klavehn (2002) report that, "This outcome is presumably attributable to the adoption of very lenient response criteria combined with the effects of greater response opportunity" (p. 91).#

Another relevant quote: "The high levels of remembering following the semantic study task resulted in many more guesses to lures than to targets. This suggests that there may be circumstances in which, when subjects include guesses in their know responses and false alarm rates are very high, high levels of remembering could result in a 'negative discrimination' effect: know responses to lures could exceed know responses to targets" (Gardiner, Java, and Richardson-Klavehn, 1996, p. 117).#

Puzzling over calibration curves#

So I've put together an unusual type of calibration curve for Experiment 1 of the DeSoto and Roediger (2014) paper. In fact, I've spent all morning thinking about it. Here's what it looks like.#

#

In my experiment, subjects make a confidence rating from 0 (not at all confident) to 100 (entirely confident) after making a recognition decision (i.e., "old" or "new"). We divide up the confidence scale into new responses and old responses. Then, perfect calibration means expected accuracy with a zero confidence rating is .50. (Previously, when we lumped "old" and "new" responses together, expected accuracy was zero. Or wait, maybe it wasn't.)#

I need to do a bit more research on calibration curves in general to understand why I'm getting tangled up in what should be a straightforward concept.#

Talked to Diana and Alex about R/K/G#

I talked to Diana and Alex in the Dobbins lab about why I might be getting the strange RKG data described earlier today. The consensus is that a situation could exist where there's a low correct guess rate. It's challenging to explain in print, but basically if the criterion for guesses is at a point at the intersection between the target and lure curves where there are more guesses for lures than targets, you'd get the results I showed earlier today. #

Both of them mentioned that using categorized lists may cause patterns that normally don't arise in signal detection studies. This is certainly something to think about.#
