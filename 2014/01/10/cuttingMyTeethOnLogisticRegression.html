Cutting my teeth on logistic regression

I'm studying up on how logistic regression works for my dissertation. My grasp is not quite as solid as I'd like it to be, but it's been good practice trying to put together regression equations for the pilot data from Experiment 2 of my dissertation.

The results, though, are quite promising. Take a look at the following figure.

<="" img="">

This figure shows the predicted accuracy for confidence ratings 0-100 (in bins of 10) as a function of whether the memory is remembered, known, or guessed. And what you can see is that increases in confidence are more predictive of increases in accuracy when memories are remembered. This is the central issue of my dissertation (or the proposal, at least).

What's somewhat concerning is that the predicted accuracy for most confidence ratings is below 50%, or chance. I need to figure out why this is happening. Basically, when people are saying "old," more than half of the time they're wrong -- which is indicative of reverse discrimination, which just doesn't seem right. I need to continue to think this issue through to determine whether this is surprising or not.
